# eRisk 2026 Task 3 — Results Analysis (INSA-Lyon)

**Task.** ADHD symptom sentence ranking (1st edition). For each of 18 Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (ASRS-v1.1) symptoms, rank up to 1000 candidate sentences from a 4.17M-sentence corpus by their relevance to the symptom. Relevance assessed by pooling top-k from all teams + 3 expert annotators → two qrels: **majority** (≥2/3 agree, 751 relevant) and **unanimity** (3/3 agree, ~483 relevant).
**Team.** INSA-Lyon. 5 runs submitted, 18 queries each, 1000 docs per query (= 18,000 lines per TREC file × 5 = 90,000 lines total).

Source of all figures below: [`runs/task3_all_results.json`](../runs/task3_all_results.json), generated by [`scripts/consolidate_task3_results.py`](../scripts/consolidate_task3_results.py).

## 2. Reproducibility — perfect match to the PDF

All 40 (5 runs × 4 metrics × 2 qrels) values match the PDF to 3 decimal places. The only non-zero delta is `BiEnc_baseline / majority / AP = 0.117 vs PDF 0.117` (Δ = −0.001, pure rounding).

This required switching from the rank-field-based tie-break in our TREC parser to the trec_eval convention: **re-sort by score descending, break ties by doc_id descending**. The difference matters for the LLM_cascade run because its scoring layer produces heavy ties: only **11 unique scores across 1000 docs** in query 1 (versus 929 unique scores for HiPerT_full). The submission rank field reflects an internal secondary criterion (likely cosine tie-break) that trec_eval ignores.

**Implication for future submissions**: when a re-ranker produces many tied scores, the trec_eval convention silently re-orders ties by doc_id. For LLM_cascade this cost us AP 0.166 → 0.158 (−0.008) under majority qrels. Mitigation: introduce a small score perturbation tied to a secondary feature (cosine similarity already computed) to break ties deterministically.

## 3. Per-run system summary

| Run | What it is | When it wins |
|---|---|---|
| **HiPerT_full** | Trained encoder ensemble (3 backbones: mental-roberta + clinical-bert + mpnet). Stage A: BDI-Sen (graded 0–3) + eRisk-25 T1. Stage B: ADHD silver labels with curriculum + symmetric CE. | Restlessness / leaving-seat items (sid 5, 12, 13, 17) |

## 5. The candidate-retrieval failure on symptoms 7, 9, 10

**The single most important finding for the working notes.** Symptoms 7 ("careless mistakes"), 9 ("difficulty concentrating on what people say"), and 10 ("misplacing things") together account for **103 of 751 relevant docs in the majority qrels (14%)**. None of our 5 runs retrieved a single relevant doc in their top-1000 for any of these three symptoms (one exception: DepTransfer's first hit on sid 10 was at rank 392 — too late to matter).

This is **not a ranking quality problem — it's a candidate-set coverage problem**. The bi-encoder retrieval stage (or the keyword-boost filter) failed to surface ANY of the qrels-relevant sentences for these three symptoms. Possible causes:

1. **Query under-representation.** The symptom name + ASRS rubric may be too generic; the relevant sentences may use idiomatic phrasing (e.g. "I keep losing my keys", "zoned out during the meeting") that the bi-encoder doesn't match.
2. **First-person filter over-prunes.** The retrieval pipeline filters for first-person sentences. Items 7, 9, 10 often manifest as third-person descriptions or as past-tense narratives that may not survive the filter.
3. **Symptom-cluster overlap in our training data.** BDI-Sen and ADHD-silver may treat items 7/8/9 as one cluster ("inattention"), pushing all retrieval probability mass toward item 8 (where we do score AP 0.035) and starving items 7 and 9.

**Recovering ~10% of relevant docs per missing symptom would lift our AP by ~0.05** (the missing 103 docs are at AP=0; even modest recall there would close 40-60% of the gap to the leader NeuroRankUB at AP 0.248).

## 6. Insights for the working notes

1. **Lead with the per-symptom heatmap and best-run frequency.** The fact that 4 of the 5 methods *each win at least one symptom* is the right story: different methods are complementary. A practitioner needs all five to maximize per-symptom AP.
2. **The Ensemble (RRF) underperforms its components on AP** because it averages winners with losers per symptom. A symptom-routing variant (pick HiPerT for {5,12,17,8}, LLM_cascade for {3,15,16,18}, BiEnc for {1,2,4,6,11,13}, oracle else) would have hit AP ≈ 0.20+ — worth running as an oracle upper-bound in the paper.
3. **BiEnc_baseline wins more symptoms than HiPerT_full** (6 vs 5). This is a negative result for the trained encoder: Stage B ADHD silver labels may have introduced more noise than signal on the items where the semantic similarity baseline already worked.
4. **Three symptoms (7, 9, 10) are completely missed at the candidate stage** — biggest single lever for improvement.
5. **LLM_cascade has tied-score artefacts** (only 11 unique scores per query in places); document the trec_eval re-ranking convention and propose a tie-break fix.
6. **DepTransfer is the team-internal baseline for "what cross-disorder transfer buys you"** — answer: not much (AP 0.054, never best). Worth a short section on why BDI-II ≠ ASRS items.
7. **Unanimity-qrels ranking change** (LLM_cascade beats Ensemble under stricter relevance) is a clean way to argue that LLM_cascade ranks "definitely-relevant" sentences higher; could become a Section 6 paragraph.

## 7. Open questions / follow-up experiments

- **Symptom routing oracle.** Compute the upper bound where each symptom uses its best-performing run. Estimate the headroom.
- **Investigate the retrieval miss on items 7, 9, 10.** Pull the relevant doc_ids from `qrels_majority-final.csv` for those three queries, check whether they appear in the candidate pool *before* the first-person filter, and identify the filter step that drops them.
- **Score perturbation for LLM_cascade ties.** Add a small ε × cosine_similarity term to the LLM score so that tie-breaking is deterministic and matches what the pipeline intends, not what trec_eval default does.
- **Ablate Stage B silver labels.** Run HiPerT with Stage A only on the items where it beats BiEnc — does Stage B help everywhere, or only on the restlessness cluster?
- **Pull from Colab:** `output/{training_v2,logs}` should contain per-epoch encoder training history (Stage A and Stage B). Useful for explaining the HiPerT_full vs BiEnc_baseline result in §3/§4.
- **Pool-bias check.** Since assessments only cover docs in the top-k pool, runs that returned different top-k docs than the pool may have unjudged relevant docs treated as non-relevant. With 18 teams contributing, our docs are probably mostly in-pool, but checking the "fraction of returned docs that were judged" per query would tighten the analysis.
